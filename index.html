FREDERICK DOUGLASS

A Tribute to an American Leader

Image of Frederick Douglass

Frederick Douglass was one of the foremost leaders of the abolitionist movement, which fought to end slavery within the United States in the decades prior to the Civil War. A brilliant speaker, Douglass was asked by the American Anti-Slavery Society to engage in a tour of lectures, and so became recognized as one of America’s first great black speakers. He won world fame when his autobiography was publicized in 1845. Two years later he began publishing an antislavery paper called "The North Star".

Douglass served as an adviser to President Abraham Lincoln during the Civil War and fought for the adoption of constitutional amendments that guaranteed voting rights and other civil liberties for African-Americans. Douglass provided a powerful voice for human rights during this period of American history and is still revered today for his contributions against injustice.

“Once you learn to read, you will be forever free.”

— Frederick Douglass

LIFE EVENTS

1838 - With borrowed documents from a sailor escaped from slavery using the Underground Railroad and headed to New York. His final destination was New Bedford, Massachusetts where he changed his last name to Douglass, a character from the poem “Lady of the Lake” by Sir Walter Scott.

1841 - Became an agent of the Massachusetts Antislavery Society giving lectures in the eastern counties of Massachusetts and called to advocate equal rights for the proposed new constitution of Rhode Island.

1845 - Published his first autobiography : The Narrative of the Life of Frederick Douglass. He left the country Aboard the Cambria and sought refuge in England to avoid being recaptured as a fugitive slave. He toured England, Ireland, Scotland and Wales meeting Ellen Richardson and Henry Richardson who raised funds to buy Douglass’ freedom.

1847 - Raised $2500 to publish a weekly newspaper The North Star. in Rochester.

1848 - The first women’s rights convention took place at Seneca Falls, NY. Douglass advocated for women’s right to vote. Began sheltering fugitive slaves in his house and became part of the branch of the Underground Railway that dispatched passengers from Rochester to Canada.

1851 - Circulation of "The North Star." doubled and the publication was changed to "Frederick Douglass’ Paper" to differentiate itself from the other “star” publications.

1855 - Douglass published his second autobiography My Bondage and My Freedom.

“Without a struggle, there can be no progress.”

— Frederick Douglass

1870 - Founded and edited the New National Era, a journal in Washington DC.

1871 - President Grant appointed Douglass as part of a commission to study the viability of annexing Santo Domingo.

1877 - Appointed as U.S Marshall of the District of Columbia by President Rutherford Hayes.

1878 - Purchased Cedar Hill in Anascotia, Washington, D.C. Today it is the Frederick Douglass National Historical Site administered by the National Park Service.

1881 - Appointed Recorder of Deeds for the District of Columbia by President Garfield. Published his third and last autobiography The Life and Times of Frederick Douglass.

1888 - Appointed as Consul General to Haiti by President Benjamin Harrison.

1892 - The Haitian government selected Douglass as commissioner to the World’s Columbian Exposition in Chicago to celebrate the 400th anniversary of Christopher Columbus’ arrival in the New World.

1893 - Douglass constructed affordable rental housing for colored people known as Douglass Place in Fells Point, Baltimore.

1895 - Attended meeting of the National Council of Women in Washington D.C. After Douglass returned home he died of a heart attack. His funeral was held at the Metropolitan African Methodist Episcopal Church and was buried in the Douglass family plot in Mount Hope Cemetery in Rochester, New York.

“Power concedes nothing without a demand. It never did and it never will.”

— Frederick Douglass
